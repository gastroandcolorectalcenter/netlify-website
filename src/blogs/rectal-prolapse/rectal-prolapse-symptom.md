Rectal prolapse is a medical condition that many people feel embarrassed to discuss, yet it affects thousands of people in Nepal and worldwide. Understanding this condition is the first step toward getting proper treatment and improving your quality of life. This comprehensive guide will help you recognize the symptoms, understand the causes, and know when to seek medical help.

## What Is Rectal Prolapse?

Rectal prolapse is a condition in which the rectum (the last portion of the large intestine) loses its normal attachments inside the body, allowing it to telescope or protrude through the anus. In simple terms, part or all of the rectal wall slides out of place and can extend outside the body.

<div class="flash-card" style="background: #e8f5e9; padding: 20px; border-left: 4px solid #4caf50; margin: 20px 0;">

**ЁЯТб Understanding the Anatomy**

The rectum is the final 15-20 cm of your large intestine, located just above the anus. Normally, it's held in place by muscles, ligaments, and other supportive tissues of the pelvic floor. When these supports weaken, prolapse can occur.

</div>

### Types of Rectal Prolapse

**1. Complete Prolapse (Full-Thickness Prolapse)**
- The entire wall of the rectum protrudes through the anus
- Most common type
- Visible tissue extends outside the body
- Also called "external prolapse" or "true prolapse"

**2. Partial Prolapse (Mucosal Prolapse)**
- Only the inner lining (mucosa) of the rectum protrudes
- Less severe than complete prolapse
- May occur during bowel movements

**3. Internal Prolapse (Internal Intussusception)**
- The rectum collapses but doesn't protrude outside
- Not visible externally
- Can cause similar symptoms
- May progress to complete prolapse if untreated

## Who Gets Rectal Prolapse?

### Common Demographics

**Women Over 50:**
- 6 times more common in women than men
- Often related to childbirth and weakened pelvic floor
- Hormonal changes after menopause may contribute

**Elderly Patients:**
- Most common in people over 60
- Age-related weakening of muscles and ligaments
- Multiple risk factors accumulate over time

**Children (Rare):**
- Usually occurs before age 4
- Often resolves with conservative treatment
- Different from adult prolapse

### Risk Factors

Understanding what increases your risk can help with prevention and early detection:

**Chronic Straining:**
- Chronic constipation
- Prolonged straining during bowel movements
- Frequent diarrhea
- Long-term cough (chronic lung disease)

**Pregnancy and Childbirth:**
- Multiple pregnancies
- Prolonged labor
- Traumatic deliveries
- Large babies
- Instrumental delivery (forceps, vacuum)

**Weakened Pelvic Floor:**
- Previous pelvic surgery
- Aging and tissue weakening
- Hormonal changes (menopause)
- Connective tissue disorders

**Neurological Conditions:**
- Spinal cord injury
- Multiple sclerosis
- Stroke
- Paralysis
- Nerve damage

**Other Medical Conditions:**
- Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
- Cystic fibrosis
- Chronic lung disease
- Parasitic infections (especially in children)

**Anatomical Factors:**
- Elongated colon
- Loose attachments of rectum
- Weak anal sphincter
- Previous rectal surgery

<div class="flash-card" style="background: #fff3e0; padding: 20px; border-left: 4px solid #ff9800; margin: 20px 0;">

**тЪая╕П Important Note**

Having one or more risk factors doesn't mean you will definitely develop rectal prolapse. However, awareness helps you take preventive measures and seek early medical attention if symptoms appear.

## Recognizing the Symptoms

Early recognition of symptoms is crucial for timely treatment. Rectal prolapse symptoms can range from mild to severe and may develop gradually or suddenly.

### Primary Symptoms

**1. Visible Tissue Protruding from Anus**
- Red, moist tissue extending outside
- May appear during bowel movements initially
- Later may occur with standing, walking, or coughing
- Can retract on its own or require manual reduction
- Size varies from small (few centimeters) to large (10+ cm)

**Appearance:**
- Red or dark pink tissue
- Moist, mucus-covered surface
- Circular or donut-shaped
- May have concentric rings
- Can bleed if irritated

**2. Feeling of Something Coming Out**
- Sensation of bulge or fullness at anus
- Feeling of incomplete evacuation
- Pressure in the pelvic area
- Heaviness or dragging sensation

**3. Mucus Discharge**
- Constant or intermittent mucus leakage
- May stain underwear
- Can cause skin irritation
- Often accompanied by moisture

### Secondary Symptoms

**Bowel-Related Symptoms:**

**Fecal Incontinence:**
- Inability to control bowel movements
- Accidental leakage of stool
- Loss of urgency sensation
- Difficulty distinguishing gas from stool
- Ranges from minor soiling to complete loss of control

**Constipation:**
- Difficulty passing stool
- Need for excessive straining
- Hard, dry stools
- Incomplete evacuation feeling
- May require digital assistance

**Rectal Bleeding:**
- Bright red blood from rectum
- Blood on toilet paper
- Blood in stool
- Due to tissue trauma or ulceration
- Usually small amounts

**Pain and Discomfort:**
- Anal or rectal pain
- Increases with prolonged prolapse
- Worse when sitting
- Cramping sensation
- Lower abdominal discomfort

**Urinary Symptoms:**
- Urinary incontinence
- Difficulty urinating
- Frequent urination
- Incomplete bladder emptying
- Due to associated pelvic floor weakness

**Impact on Daily Life:**
- Difficulty sitting comfortably
- Problems with personal hygiene
- Skin irritation and soreness
- Embarrassment and social withdrawal
- Anxiety and depression
- Reduced physical activity
- Sexual dysfunction

<div class="flash-card" style="background: #e3f2fd; padding: 20px; border-left: 4px solid #2196f3; margin: 20px 0;">

**ЁЯУК Symptom Progression**

Rectal prolapse typically progresses through stages:

**Early Stage:** Prolapse only during bowel movements, returns spontaneously

**Middle Stage:** Prolapse with straining, standing, or walking; may need manual reduction

**Advanced Stage:** Prolapse is persistent, difficult to reduce, and may become irreducible

</div>

### Symptoms That Require Immediate Medical Attention

<div class="flash-card" style="background: #fce4ec; padding: 20px; border-left: 4px solid #e91e63; margin: 20px 0;">

**ЁЯЪи Emergency Warning Signs**

Seek immediate medical care if you experience:

- **Strangulated prolapse:** Prolapse that cannot be pushed back and becomes dark, purple, or black
- **Severe pain:** Intense, unrelenting pain in the rectal area
- **Heavy bleeding:** Significant blood loss from the rectum
- **Signs of infection:** Fever, severe swelling, foul-smelling discharge
- **Complete inability to pass stool or gas:** May indicate obstruction
- **Tissue death (necrosis):** Blackened, foul-smelling tissue

These symptoms may indicate complications requiring urgent surgical intervention.

</div>

## How Rectal Prolapse Develops

Understanding the progression can help you recognize early signs:

### Stage 1: Weakening of Supports
- Pelvic floor muscles gradually weaken
- Ligaments stretch
- Often no symptoms yet
- Changes may be happening over years

### Stage 2: Internal Changes
- Rectum begins to telescope internally
- May feel incomplete evacuation
- Mild constipation or straining
- Internal intussusception present

### Stage 3: Intermittent Prolapse
- Tissue protrudes during bowel movements
- Returns spontaneously afterward
- Increasing frequency over time
- Beginning of visible symptoms

### Stage 4: Progressive Prolapse
- Prolapse occurs with less provocation
- Standing, coughing, or walking triggers it
- May require manual reduction
- Growing size and duration

### Stage 5: Persistent Prolapse
- Prolapse remains outside most or all the time
- Difficult or impossible to reduce
- Severe symptoms
- High risk of complications

## Distinguishing Rectal Prolapse from Similar Conditions

Several conditions can be confused with rectal prolapse:

### Hemorrhoids (Piles)
**Differences:**
- Hemorrhoids are swollen blood vessels
- Have grape-like appearance
- Usually painful
- Don't have concentric rings
- Bleed more readily
- Occur in specific positions (internal or external)

**Similarities:**
- Can protrude from anus
- May cause bleeding
- Occur with straining

### Rectal Polyps
**Differences:**
- Usually smaller
- Attached by stalk
- Smooth surface
- Don't have layers
- Often asymptomatic

### Anal Skin Tags
**Differences:**
- Small, soft tissue growths
- Painless
- Don't change size
- Don't retract

<div class="flash-card" style="background: #e8f5e9; padding: 20px; border-left: 4px solid #4caf50; margin: 20px 0;">

**ЁЯФН Key Distinguishing Feature**

Rectal prolapse shows concentric circular rings or folds (like a "target" or "bulls-eye" pattern), while hemorrhoids appear as distinct masses or grape-like clusters. If you're unsure, always consult a colorectal surgeon for proper diagnosis.

</div>

## What to Expect at Your Doctor's Visit

### Medical History
Your doctor will ask about:
- Duration and frequency of symptoms
- What triggers the prolapse
- Bowel movement patterns
- Previous pregnancies and deliveries
- Medical conditions
- Medications
- Impact on daily activities

### Physical Examination
**Visual Inspection:**
- Examination of prolapse at rest
- Assessment during straining
- Evaluation of prolapse size and appearance

**Digital Rectal Examination:**
- Assessment of sphincter tone
- Evaluation of pelvic floor strength
- Checking for masses or abnormalities

**Straining Test:**
- Asked to strain as if having bowel movement
- Helps demonstrate prolapse
- Assesses severity

### Diagnostic Tests (If Needed)
- Colonoscopy or sigmoidoscopy
- Defecography (special X-ray during defecation)
- Anorectal manometry (measures sphincter pressure)
- Pelvic floor imaging (MRI)
- Transit studies

## Living with Rectal Prolapse: Immediate Management

While awaiting treatment, these measures can help:

### Managing Prolapse Episodes

**Manual Reduction:**
- Wash hands thoroughly
- Use lubricant (petroleum jelly or water-based)
- Gently push tissue back
- Lie down if possible
- Apply gentle, steady pressure
- Don't force if won't go back

**Preventing Episodes:**
- Avoid straining
- Don't delay bowel movements
- Maintain soft stools
- Support pelvic floor when standing
- Avoid heavy lifting

### Symptom Relief

**For Mucus Discharge:**
- Use absorbent pads
- Frequent gentle cleaning
- Barrier creams to protect skin
- Change underwear regularly

**For Skin Irritation:**
- Gentle cleaning with water
- Pat dry, don't rub
- Use barrier creams
- Avoid harsh soaps
- Cotton underwear

**For Constipation:**
- Increase fiber intake
- Drink plenty of water
- Regular meal times
- Gentle exercise
- Stool softeners if needed

## Myths and Facts About Rectal Prolapse

### Myth 1: "It's just hemorrhoids"
**Fact:** While they can look similar, rectal prolapse is a different, more serious condition requiring different treatment.

### Myth 2: "It will go away on its own"
**Fact:** Rectal prolapse doesn't resolve without treatment and typically worsens over time.

### Myth 3: "Only elderly people get it"
**Fact:** While more common in older adults, rectal prolapse can occur at any age, including in children.

### Myth 4: "Surgery is always dangerous"
**Fact:** Modern surgical techniques are safe and effective with low complication rates when performed by experienced surgeons.

### Myth 5: "I caused this by straining too hard"
**Fact:** While straining is a risk factor, rectal prolapse results from multiple factors, many beyond your control.

### Myth 6: "There's nothing that can be done"
**Fact:** Effective treatments are available, including both surgical and non-surgical options depending on severity.

## Prevention Strategies

While not all cases can be prevented, these strategies can reduce your risk:

### Maintain Healthy Bowel Habits
- Don't strain during bowel movements
- Don't delay when you feel the urge
- Spend limited time on toilet
- Avoid reading or phone use while toileting

### Dietary Measures
- Eat high-fiber diet (vegetables, fruits, whole grains)
- Drink adequate water (8-10 glasses daily)
- Avoid excessive processed foods
- Regular meal times

### Pelvic Floor Health
- Pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises)
- Proper lifting techniques
- Maintain healthy weight
- Exercise regularly

### Manage Risk Factors
- Treat constipation promptly
- Control chronic cough
- Manage chronic lung conditions
- Seek treatment for chronic diarrhea
